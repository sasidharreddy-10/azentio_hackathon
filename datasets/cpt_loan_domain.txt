================================================================================
LOAN PORTFOLIO & CREDIT RISK — DOMAIN KNOWLEDGE CORPUS
CPT Training Text | Retail Lending, Underwriting & Collections
================================================================================

This corpus covers all fields used in a retail loan dataset, including their
definitions, industry context, calculation methods, thresholds, and
relationships to other variables. It is intended to build foundational
knowledge of how lenders originate, monitor, and recover loans in the Indian
retail credit market.

================================================================================
SECTION 1: LOAN IDENTIFICATION FIELDS
================================================================================

--- LOAN_ID ---

LOAN_ID is the unique alphanumeric identifier assigned to each individual loan
account at the time of origination. No two loan accounts in a portfolio will
share the same LOAN_ID. It is the primary key used to track a loan through its
entire lifecycle — from application and disbursal through repayment, default,
or closure.

In practice, LOAN_ID is generated by the lender's loan origination system (LOS)
or core banking system (CBS) immediately upon sanction or disbursal. The format
varies by institution: some use sequential numeric strings (e.g., LN000012345),
while others encode metadata such as product type, branch code, or year of
origination in the ID (e.g., PL-MUM-2024-00781).

LOAN_ID is used in:
- Loan management systems (LMS) to pull account-level details
- Collections systems to assign delinquent accounts to agents
- Bureau reporting to CIBIL, Equifax, Experian, or CRIF High Mark
- MIS (Management Information System) reports for portfolio monitoring
- Legal and recovery proceedings when a loan is referred for action

LOAN_ID is distinct from APPLICATION_ID, which is assigned at the point of
application before a decision is made. Once a loan is sanctioned and disbursed,
a LOAN_ID is generated. The application may be rejected, in which case no
LOAN_ID is created.

--- CUSTOMER_ID ---

CUSTOMER_ID is the unique identifier assigned to each borrower (individual or
entity) within the lender's customer master. Unlike LOAN_ID which is
account-level, CUSTOMER_ID is customer-level. A single CUSTOMER_ID can be
associated with multiple LOAN_IDs, because one borrower may hold more than one
loan product simultaneously or sequentially.

For example, a customer (CUSTOMER_ID: C-10045) may have:
- A Personal Loan (LOAN_ID: PL-2022-00124) that was closed in 2023
- A Business Loan (LOAN_ID: BL-2023-00789) currently active
- A Consumer Durable Loan (LOAN_ID: CD-2024-00345) recently disbursed

This one-to-many relationship between CUSTOMER_ID and LOAN_ID is central to
cross-sell analysis, customer-level exposure management, and risk concentration
limits. Lenders use CUSTOMER_ID to:
- Aggregate total indebtedness across all products (Total Exposure)
- Identify co-borrower or guarantor relationships
- Track a customer's repayment history across all accounts
- Manage bureau reporting at a customer level

In the context of credit risk modelling, features are often engineered at the
CUSTOMER_ID level — for instance, "number of active loans", "total outstanding
balance across all loans", or "worst DPD ever seen across any loan" — and
these customer-level aggregates are joined to account-level records using
CUSTOMER_ID as the join key.

================================================================================
SECTION 2: BORROWER DEMOGRAPHICS
================================================================================

--- AGE ---

AGE refers to the borrower's age in completed years at the time of loan
application or as of the data extraction date. It is one of the most
fundamental demographic variables in retail credit underwriting.

Lenders in India generally require borrowers to be between 21 and 65 years of
age at the time of loan application. Some lenders extend the upper limit to
70 for certain secured products. The minimum age ensures legal capacity to
contract, while the upper limit is linked to expected income continuity and
repayment horizon.

Age interacts with several other variables:

1. Loan Tenure: A 58-year-old borrower cannot take a 10-year loan if the
   lender's policy requires the loan to be repaid before age 65. Effective
   maximum tenure = max_age - current_age.

2. Income Stability: Salaried borrowers near retirement (age > 55) may have
   their income continuity questioned. Self-employed borrowers, on the other
   hand, may continue generating income well past 60.

3. Credit History Length: Older borrowers typically have longer credit
   histories, which generally correlates with more stable bureau scores —
   though exceptions exist.

4. Product Suitability: Consumer durable loans and two-wheeler loans are
   disproportionately taken by younger borrowers (age 21-35). Home loans and
   business loans tend to attract older, more financially established borrowers.

In predictive default models, age is often transformed:
- Binned into age bands: 21-30, 31-40, 41-50, 51-60, 60+
- Used in interaction terms with BUREAU_SCORE or VINTAGE_MONTHS
- Used to derive "age at maturity" (AGE + LOAN_TENURE/12)

Age should not be confused with VINTAGE_MONTHS (age of the loan) — these are
entirely different concepts.

--- GENDER ---

GENDER captures the borrower's gender, typically recorded as Male, Female, or
Other/Third Gender. In operational datasets, GENDER values are often messy due
to data entry inconsistencies: "M", "Male", "MALE", "m", "F", "Female",
"FEMALE", "f", "Others", "O" may all appear in the same column and must be
standardised during data preparation.

Normalisation steps typically include:
- Uppercasing all values
- Mapping M / MALE → Male
- Mapping F / FEMALE → Female
- Mapping O / OTHER / OTHERS / THIRD GENDER → Other
- Treating NaN / NULL / blank as Unknown

In Indian credit underwriting, GENDER is not a permissible factor in credit
decisions under the Reserve Bank of India's (RBI) fair lending guidelines —
lenders cannot approve or reject a loan or price it differently based on gender.
However, gender is retained in datasets for:
- Portfolio composition analysis (e.g., "what % of our disbursals are to women")
- Government scheme eligibility (Mudra loans, PMEGP, etc. often have gender
  targets)
- Credit inclusion and financial inclusion studies
- Default rate analysis segmented by gender (for research, not underwriting)

Empirical studies in the Indian market have generally shown that female
borrowers exhibit slightly lower default rates than male borrowers at comparable
bureau score levels, but this relationship is confounded by product mix,
geography, and income level.

--- STATE ---

STATE captures the Indian state or union territory of the borrower's registered
address. India has 28 states and 8 union territories. In loan datasets, STATE
is used to:

1. Geographic Risk Analysis: Some states have historically higher default rates
   due to agricultural distress, economic concentration in a single sector, or
   natural disaster vulnerability.

2. Regulatory Compliance: Certain state governments have moratorium orders,
   interest rate caps, or waiver schemes that affect recovery and collections.
   For example, farm loan waiver schemes in Maharashtra, Uttar Pradesh, and
   Punjab directly affect agricultural loan portfolios.

3. Product Penetration: Urban-heavy states like Maharashtra, Karnataka, Tamil
   Nadu, and Delhi tend to have high penetration of personal loans and consumer
   durables. Rural-heavy states have more agricultural and MSME loan exposure.

4. Collections Strategy: Field collection agent allocation, legal recovery costs,
   and Lok Adalat availability vary by state.

5. Regulatory Jurisdiction: Debt Recovery Tribunal (DRT) and Debt Recovery
   Appellate Tribunal (DRAT) jurisdiction is state-based.

Common states in Indian retail loan portfolios include: Maharashtra, Uttar
Pradesh, Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, West Bengal, Rajasthan, Gujarat, Andhra Pradesh,
Telangana, Madhya Pradesh, Bihar, and Delhi (NCT). Smaller portfolios or
specialist lenders may have concentration in specific states.

--- OCCUPATION ---

OCCUPATION captures the borrower's primary source of livelihood or employment
category. It is one of the most important variables in income verification,
loan eligibility, and repayment risk assessment.

Common occupation categories in Indian retail lending:

Salaried: Borrower receives a fixed monthly salary from an employer.
Sub-categories include:
  - Salaried - Government: Central or state government employee. Lowest default
    risk due to job security and pension.
  - Salaried - PSU: Public Sector Undertaking employee. Similar stability.
  - Salaried - Private (Large Company): Employee of a listed or large private
    corporation. Moderate risk; job security depends on company health.
  - Salaried - Private (SME): Employee of a small or mid-size firm. Higher
    income volatility.

Self-Employed Professional (SEP): Doctors, lawyers, CAs, architects with
professional degrees running their own practice. Income is predictable but
requires Income Tax Returns (ITR) or professional income proof.

Self-Employed Non-Professional (SENP): Traders, shopkeepers, small business
owners, contractors. Highest income volatility and documentation challenges.

Agriculturalist / Farmer: Income is seasonal and crop-dependent. Subject to
agricultural distress.

Housewife / Homemaker: No independent income source; often a co-borrower with
a salaried primary borrower.

Student: Very limited borrowing capacity; typical for education loans.
Retired: Fixed pension income; loan tenure must end before income risk rises.
Daily Wage / Labour: Highly irregular income; very high credit risk.

In underwriting, OCCUPATION determines:
- Which income documents are acceptable (salary slip vs. ITR vs. bank statement)
- Stability multiplier applied to stated income
- FOIR (Fixed Obligation to Income Ratio) computation
- Sector-specific concentration limits

================================================================================
SECTION 3: INCOME AND FINANCIAL CAPACITY
================================================================================

--- MONTHLY_INCOME ---

MONTHLY_INCOME is the declared or verified monthly income of the borrower at
the time of loan application. It is stated in Indian Rupees (₹) and represents
gross income before taxes and deductions, or net take-home income depending on
the lender's policy.

For salaried borrowers, MONTHLY_INCOME is typically derived from:
- Latest 3 months' salary slips (average taken)
- Bank account credit entries for salary credits
- Form 16 (annual salary certificate) divided by 12

For self-employed borrowers, MONTHLY_INCOME is derived from:
- Income Tax Return (ITR) — Net Profit divided by 12
- Gross Turnover from GST returns divided by 12, then applying a profit margin
- Bank statement average monthly credit (ABMC) as a proxy

MONTHLY_INCOME is central to several underwriting calculations:

1. FOIR (Fixed Obligation to Income Ratio):
   FOIR = (Sum of all monthly EMI obligations) / MONTHLY_INCOME
   Typical acceptable FOIR: 40%-55% for salaried, 50%-65% for self-employed.
   Higher FOIR indicates the borrower is more leveraged and has less disposable
   income to handle new obligations.

2. EMI Eligibility:
   Maximum EMI the borrower can service = MONTHLY_INCOME × (1 - FOIR_threshold)

3. Loan Amount Eligibility:
   LOAN_AMOUNT = Maximum affordable EMI × Annuity factor for given tenure and rate.

4. Disposable Income:
   DISPOSABLE_INCOME = MONTHLY_INCOME - Existing fixed obligations
   This is sometimes shown separately in underwriting scorecards.

Income declared at origination may differ from actual income during the loan
lifecycle due to job loss, salary cuts, business downturns, or promotions. This
is why lenders track repayment behaviour (DPD patterns) and not just origination
income.

Income bands commonly used in Indian retail lending:
- Low income: < ₹20,000/month
- Lower-middle income: ₹20,000 – ₹50,000/month
- Middle income: ₹50,000 – ₹1,00,000/month
- Upper-middle income: ₹1,00,000 – ₹3,00,000/month
- High income: > ₹3,00,000/month

SECTION 4: LOAN PRODUCT AND STRUCTURE
================================================================================

--- LOAN_PRODUCT ---

LOAN_PRODUCT specifies the type of credit facility extended to the borrower.
Each product has distinct underwriting criteria, purpose, tenure ranges, and
risk characteristics. Common loan products in Indian retail portfolios:

PERSONAL LOAN (PL):
  Unsecured term loan for any personal purpose (medical, travel, marriage,
  education, home renovation). No collateral required. Typically ₹50,000 to
  ₹50 lakhs. Tenure: 12 to 60 months. Interest rate: 10%–24% per annum.
  Highest interest rate due to unsecured nature. Fastest to process (1–3 days).
  Default risk is moderate to high without collateral.

BUSINESS LOAN (BL):
  Unsecured or secured loan to meet working capital or expansion needs of a
  business. Borrower must demonstrate business vintage (typically 2+ years) and
  provide business financials. Ticket size: ₹1 lakh to ₹50 lakhs typically.
  Income proof: ITR, GST returns, bank statements.

HOME LOAN (HL):
  Secured loan against the property being purchased or constructed. Lowest
  interest rate (7%–10% typically) due to collateral. Longest tenure (up to
  30 years). Largest ticket sizes (₹10 lakhs to several crores). LTV
  (Loan-to-Value) ratio capped by RBI guidelines (typically 75%-90% depending
  on loan size). Default risk is lower due to security, but recovery timelines
  are long.

LOAN AGAINST PROPERTY (LAP):
  Secured loan where borrower's existing residential or commercial property is
  pledged as collateral. Higher LTV risk than home loan because funds are used
  for general business or personal purposes — not tied to the asset. Tenure:
  5 to 20 years.

CONSUMER DURABLE LOAN (CD):
  Point-of-sale financing for electronics, appliances (TV, refrigerator, mobile,
  laptop). Very small ticket (₹5,000 to ₹2 lakhs). Short tenure (3–24 months).
  Often zero-cost EMI schemes where the interest is borne by the manufacturer
  or retailer. High volume, thin margins, fast decisioning.

TWO-WHEELER LOAN (TW):
  Secured auto loan for purchase of motorcycles or scooters. Vehicle is the
  collateral. Ticket: ₹40,000 to ₹2 lakhs. Tenure: 12–48 months.

EDUCATION LOAN:
  For higher education expenses. Repayment typically begins 6–12 months after
  course completion (moratorium period). Often has government interest subsidy
  for economically weaker sections.

GOLD LOAN:
  Loan against gold ornaments as collateral. Fastest disbursal (often same day).
  LTV typically 75% of gold value. Short tenure (3–12 months). Very low default
  rate due to high-quality liquid collateral.

MICROFINANCE LOAN (MFI):
  Very small loans (₹5,000 to ₹1 lakh) to economically weaker sections, often
  women in rural/semi-urban areas through Self-Help Groups (SHG) or Joint
  Liability Groups (JLG). Regulated under NBFC-MFI guidelines of RBI.

--- LOAN_AMOUNT ---

LOAN_AMOUNT is the total credit amount requested by the borrower at the time of
application. It represents what the borrower wants, not necessarily what will
be approved. LOAN_AMOUNT is the starting point of the underwriting process.

LOAN_AMOUNT determination from the borrower's perspective:
- Based on the purpose of the loan (cost of asset being financed, or fund
  requirement for business expansion)
- Subject to the borrower's own repayment capacity estimate

From the lender's perspective, LOAN_AMOUNT requested is evaluated against:
- Borrower's income and FOIR constraints
- Bureau score thresholds
- Product-specific minimum and maximum limits
- LTV ratio (for secured loans: LOAN_AMOUNT ≤ LTV% × Asset Value)
- Prior credit behaviour of the borrower

The final approved amount may be equal to, or less than, the requested
LOAN_AMOUNT. This approved amount is captured separately as SANCTION_AMOUNT.

--- SANCTION_AMOUNT ---

SANCTION_AMOUNT is the loan amount formally approved and sanctioned by the
lender after the underwriting process is complete. It may be equal to
LOAN_AMOUNT (full approval) or lower (partial approval / counter-offer) based
on the underwriter's assessment of the borrower's repayment capacity and risk.

Sanction vs. Disbursal:
  SANCTION_AMOUNT is the approved limit. DISBURSAL_AMOUNT is the amount actually
  released to the borrower. In most retail loans, sanction and disbursal amounts
  are the same. In certain products (like construction home loans or overdraft
  facilities), sanction and disbursal can differ — the borrower draws down
  funds in tranches.

Sanction Rate:
  SANCTION_AMOUNT / LOAN_AMOUNT × 100 = Sanction Rate (%)
  A sanction rate below 100% indicates a counter-offer was made.
  Portfolio-level sanction rates are a key metric for underwriting stringency
  and sales-credit alignment.

--- LOAN_TENURE ---

LOAN_TENURE is the duration of the loan expressed in months — the period over
which the borrower is expected to repay the loan through EMIs.

Typical tenures by product:
  - Consumer Durable Loan: 3–24 months
  - Personal Loan: 12–60 months
  - Two-Wheeler Loan: 12–48 months
  - Business Loan: 12–60 months
  - Home Loan: 60–360 months (5–30 years)
  - Loan Against Property: 60–240 months (5–20 years)

LOAN_TENURE interacts with:
  - EMI_AMOUNT: Longer tenure → lower EMI → more affordable monthly payment,
    but higher total interest cost over the life of the loan.
  - INTEREST_RATE: Total interest paid = EMI × LOAN_TENURE − LOAN_AMOUNT
  - AGE at maturity: Lenders ensure the borrower does not exceed the maximum
    permissible age (typically 65–70) at loan maturity.
  - Default Risk: Empirically, longer-tenure loans have higher cumulative
    probability of encountering adverse events (job loss, illness, divorce)
    over the extended repayment window.

--- INTEREST_RATE ---

INTEREST_RATE is the annual rate of interest charged by the lender on the
outstanding loan principal. It is expressed as a percentage per annum (% p.a.)
and determines the cost of credit to the borrower.

Types of interest rate structures:
  Fixed Rate: Interest rate remains constant throughout the loan tenure.
  Floating Rate: Interest rate is linked to a benchmark (RBI Repo Rate, MCLR
    of the bank, or external benchmark). The rate changes when the benchmark
    changes, affecting EMI amount or loan tenure.
  Reducing Balance Rate: Interest is charged on the outstanding principal
    balance each month, not the original loan amount. Most retail loans use
    this method.
  Flat Rate: Interest is charged on the original loan amount for the full
    tenure, regardless of repayments. Effective rate under flat method is
    approximately 1.8× the stated flat rate. Rarely used now.

EMI Calculation (Reducing Balance Method):
  EMI = P × r × (1+r)^n / [(1+r)^n − 1]
  Where:
    P = Principal (SANCTION_AMOUNT)
    r = Monthly interest rate = INTEREST_RATE / 12 / 100
    n = LOAN_TENURE in months

Interest Rate Determinants:
  - RBI Repo Rate and monetary policy stance
  - Borrower's BUREAU_SCORE (higher score → lower rate)
  - Loan product type (secured vs. unsecured)
  - Borrower's income and occupation category
  - Lender's cost of funds and target margin

Risk-Based Pricing (RBP):
  Lenders practicing RBP offer different interest rates to different borrowers
  based on risk. A borrower with BUREAU_SCORE 780 may get 11.5% p.a. while
  a borrower with BUREAU_SCORE 680 on the same product gets 16% p.a.

--- EMI_AMOUNT ---

EMI (Equated Monthly Installment) is the fixed monthly payment made by the
borrower to the lender throughout the loan tenure. It comprises two components:
  - Principal Component: Portion that reduces the outstanding loan balance
  - Interest Component: Portion paid as cost of credit

EMI structure over time (amortisation):
  In the early months of a loan, the interest component dominates the EMI
  because the outstanding principal is high. As the loan ages and principal
  is repaid, the interest component shrinks and the principal component grows.
  The total EMI remains constant (for fixed rate loans) but its composition
  changes every month.

EMI Affordability:
  EMI / MONTHLY_INCOME × 100 = EMI-to-Income Ratio for a single loan.
  All EMIs / MONTHLY_INCOME × 100 = FOIR for all obligations combined.
  Lenders use FOIR to assess overall burden.

EMI calculation example:
  Loan: ₹3,00,000 | Tenure: 24 months | Rate: 14% p.a.
  Monthly rate r = 14/12/100 = 0.01167
  EMI = 300000 × 0.01167 × (1.01167)^24 / [(1.01167)^24 − 1]
      = 300000 × 0.01167 × 1.3247 / 0.3247
      = 300000 × 0.04758
      = ₹14,274 per month (approximately)

Missed EMI consequences:
  When a borrower fails to pay an EMI on the due date, the account enters DPD
  (Days Past Due). The lender charges a penal interest (typically 2%-3% p.a.
  extra on the overdue amount) on the overdue EMI from the due date.

SECTION 5: CREDIT BUREAU AND RISK INDICATORS
================================================================================

--- BUREAU_SCORE ---

BUREAU_SCORE is the credit score assigned to a borrower by a credit information
company (bureau) based on the borrower's historical credit behaviour across all
lenders. In India, the primary credit bureaus are:

  1. CIBIL (TransUnion CIBIL): Most widely used. Score range: 300–900.
  2. Equifax: Score range: 1–999.
  3. Experian: Score range: 300–900.
  4. CRIF High Mark: Score range: 300–900.

When lenders refer to "bureau score" without specifying the bureau, they
typically mean the CIBIL score (range 300–900).

BUREAU_SCORE interpretation (CIBIL scale):
  300–549: Very Poor — High default risk. Most lenders reject or require
           significant collateral and high interest rates.
  550–649: Poor — Above-average risk. Limited product access, high pricing.
  650–699: Fair — Marginal credit. Some lenders offer products with conditions.
  700–749: Good — Acceptable credit risk for most standard products.
  750–799: Very Good — Preferred borrower. Competitive rates offered.
  800–900: Excellent — Premium segment. Best rates and highest loan amounts.

What factors determine BUREAU_SCORE:
  Payment History (35% weight approx): On-time payments boost score; missed
    payments, DPDs, and defaults severely damage it.
  Credit Utilisation (30%): Ratio of credit card balances to credit limits.
    Utilisation below 30% is considered healthy.
  Credit History Length (15%): Longer the credit history, generally better.
  Credit Mix (10%): Having both secured (home, auto) and unsecured (personal,
    credit card) loans is seen positively.
  New Credit Inquiries (10%): Multiple hard inquiries in a short period suggest
    credit hunger and reduce the score.

Score impact of DPD:
  1–30 DPD (1 missed EMI): Moderate impact, −20 to −40 points approximately.
  31–60 DPD: Significant impact, −40 to −80 points.
  61–90 DPD: Severe impact, −80 to −120 points.
  90+ DPD (NPA): Catastrophic impact; score can drop to 300-500 range.

Special case — NH (No Hit):
  A borrower with no prior credit history has no bureau score. Lenders call this
  "NH" or "No Hit". These borrowers are either new-to-credit (NTC) or have
  only informal borrowing history. Lenders price higher to compensate for
  lack of track record. Alternative data (utility bill payments, GST returns,
  bank statement cash flows) may be used to assess NTC borrowers.

Special case — −1 Score:
  Some bureaus return −1 when there is insufficient data for a score (e.g., only
  one enquiry on file, no active trades). Lenders treat −1 similar to NH.

BUREAU_SCORE in underwriting:
  Lenders set minimum score cutoffs (e.g., minimum 700 for personal loans).
  Score is also used in risk-based pricing matrices to set INTEREST_RATE.
  Score below the cutoff results in automatic rejection in most systems.

================================================================================
SECTION 6: LOAN LIFECYCLE AND AGEING
================================================================================

--- VINTAGE_MONTHS ---

VINTAGE_MONTHS is the number of months that have elapsed since the loan was
disbursed, measured as of the data extraction date. It represents the "age" of
the loan account.

Formula: VINTAGE_MONTHS = (Data Date − DISBURSAL_DATE) in months, rounded down.

Example:
  Loan disbursed on 15-Jan-2023. Data extracted on 20-Oct-2024.
  Vintage = 21 months (approximately).

Vintage is critical in credit risk analysis because:

1. Default Timing Curve (Vintage Curve Analysis):
   Loans tend to follow predictable default timing patterns. For most retail
   unsecured loans in India, defaults peak between months 6 and 18 (the
   "peak DPD window"). Very early defaults (< 3 months) suggest fraud or
   misrepresentation. Very late defaults (> 36 months) often relate to
   life events (medical emergency, business failure).

2. Seasoning Effect:
   Loans that have successfully repaid for 12+ months are considered "seasoned"
   and carry lower marginal default risk. Credit models often include a
   seasoning variable.

3. Portfolio Vintage Analysis:
   Lenders group loans by their disbursal period (monthly or quarterly cohorts)
   and track each cohort's cumulative default rate over time. This is called
   vintage analysis or roll-rate analysis and helps identify underwriting
   quality changes over time.

4. Early Mortality Rate (EMR):
   EMR = % of loans that default within 3 months of disbursal.
   A high EMR suggests fraud, mis-selling, or poor underwriting at origination.

--- DISBURSAL_DATE ---

DISBURSAL_DATE is the date on which the sanctioned loan amount was transferred
to the borrower's account or directly to the seller / service provider (in case
of asset-linked products). This marks the formal start of the loan lifecycle.

DISBURSAL_DATE is used to calculate:
  - VINTAGE_MONTHS: How old is the loan
  - First EMI due date: Usually 30 days after DISBURSAL_DATE, though lenders
    may allow a stub period (e.g., disbursal on 15th, first EMI due on 1st of
    the month after next)
  - CURRENT_DPD: Days overdue as of today relative to the latest due date
  - Loan maturity date = DISBURSAL_DATE + LOAN_TENURE months

Disbursal date is also used for cohort-based analysis — grouping all loans
disbursed in a given month or quarter to track portfolio performance over time.

--- LAST_PAYMENT_DATE ---

LAST_PAYMENT_DATE is the date on which the borrower made the most recent
payment towards the loan account. It captures the latest activity on the
account.

LAST_PAYMENT_DATE is used to:
  1. Calculate CURRENT_DPD more precisely:
     If today is 20-Oct-2024 and the EMI was due on 01-Oct-2024, and
     LAST_PAYMENT_DATE was 15-Sep-2024 (last month's EMI), then the Oct EMI
     is unpaid → CURRENT_DPD = 19 days.

  2. Identify Dormant Accounts:
     If LAST_PAYMENT_DATE is very old (e.g., > 6 months ago) and the loan
     is not yet closed, the account is deeply delinquent or likely to be
     written off.

  3. Collections Intelligence:
     Agents use LAST_PAYMENT_DATE to understand the customer's payment pattern.
     A customer who paid 20 months regularly and then suddenly stopped is
     treated differently from one who has never paid.

  4. Partial Payment Tracking:
     LAST_PAYMENT_DATE reflects even partial payments, which are common in
     collections. A customer owing ₹15,000 EMI may pay ₹5,000 — this is
     recorded as a partial payment on LAST_PAYMENT_DATE.

================================================================================
SECTION 7: DELINQUENCY AND DEFAULT MEASUREMENT
================================================================================

--- CURRENT_DPD ---

CURRENT_DPD (Current Days Past Due) is the number of days by which the
borrower's most recent EMI payment obligation is overdue as of the reporting
or data extraction date. It is one of the most important real-time indicators
of a loan's health.

DPD Calculation:
  If an EMI was due on 1st October 2024 and has not been paid as of 25th
  October 2024, the CURRENT_DPD = 24 days.
  If the EMI was paid on 10th October (9 days late), CURRENT_DPD resets to 0
  (and MAX_DPD gets updated if 9 exceeds prior maximum).

CURRENT_DPD = 0: The account is current. Borrower is paying on time.
CURRENT_DPD 1–30: One EMI missed or partially missed. "SMA-0" or "SMA-1"
  in RBI's Special Mention Account framework.
CURRENT_DPD 31–60: Second EMI overdue. "SMA-1" bucket.
CURRENT_DPD 61–90: Third EMI overdue. "SMA-2" bucket. Loan at high risk.
CURRENT_DPD > 90: Loan classified as Non-Performing Asset (NPA) under RBI
  guidelines. "Sub-standard" classification. Lender must make provisions.

CURRENT_DPD > 90 trigger (NPA classification):
  Under RBI's Prudential Norms for Income Recognition, Asset Classification
  and Provisioning (IRACP), a loan becomes NPA when:
  - Principal or interest payment is overdue for more than 90 days.
  Once NPA, the lender stops recognising interest income on the loan ("income
  reversal"). Provisioning requirements increase significantly.

Note: CURRENT_DPD is a snapshot indicator — it reflects the status on a specific
date. Trends in CURRENT_DPD over time (monthly snapshots) are more informative
for risk assessment.

--- MAX_DPD ---

MAX_DPD (Maximum Days Past Due) is the highest number of days past due ever
observed on a loan account across its entire history, up to the reporting date.
Unlike CURRENT_DPD which may show 0 after a borrower catches up, MAX_DPD
retains the "worst ever" record and never resets downward.

Why MAX_DPD matters:
  A borrower who shows CURRENT_DPD = 0 may appear current, but if MAX_DPD = 90,
  it tells the analyst that this borrower was once 3 months delinquent. This
  historical behaviour is a strong predictor of future delinquency.

MAX_DPD is used in:
  - BUREAU_SCORE computation (payment history component)
  - Bureau trade line reporting: Each lender reports DPD to bureaus monthly
  - Re-underwriting decisions: When a borrower applies for a new loan, their
    MAX_DPD on existing or past loans is a key input
  - Collections strategy: Prior DPD history helps predict collectability

MAX_DPD interpretation:
  MAX_DPD = 0: Never missed a payment. Clean record.
  MAX_DPD 1–30: Minor slip, probably recovered quickly. Low concern.
  MAX_DPD 31–60: Moderate delinquency history. Some concern.
  MAX_DPD 61–90: Significant delinquency. High concern for new lending.
  MAX_DPD > 90: Prior NPA event. Very high risk; most lenders reject new
                applications with MAX_DPD > 90 on existing loans.

--- COLLECTION_BUCKET ---

COLLECTION_BUCKET is the delinquency classification bucket assigned to a loan
account based on its CURRENT_DPD. Buckets are used by collections teams to
prioritise and allocate accounts to appropriate resolution strategies.

Standard collection buckets in Indian retail lending:

  X (Current / 0 DPD): No overdue. No collection action needed. Preventive
    communications (payment reminders, upcoming due date alerts) may be sent.

  Bucket 1 (1–30 DPD): One EMI missed. Initial soft reminder calls and SMS.
    Target: Cure rate (payment received) within the bucket.
    Resource allocation: IVR calls, WhatsApp messages, junior agents.

  Bucket 2 (31–60 DPD): Two EMIs missed. More aggressive follow-up.
    Target: Promise-to-Pay (PTP) arrangement for full overdues.
    Resource: Experienced agents; field visits in high-ticket cases.

  Bucket 3 (61–90 DPD): Three EMIs missed. Pre-NPA stage.
    Target: One-time settlement (OTS) offers, restructuring discussions.
    Resource: Senior collections agents or supervisors.

  NPA / Bucket 4+ (91–180 DPD): Non-Performing Asset.
    Loan handed to dedicated NPA resolution team or outsourced to recovery
    agencies. Legal notices under SARFAESI Act (for secured loans) or
    civil suits may be initiated.

  Written-Off (> 180 DPD or per lender policy): Loan removed from books
    as an asset; 100% provisioned. Recovery efforts continue but are tracked
    off-balance-sheet.

Roll Rate Analysis:
  Tracking what % of accounts in each bucket "roll forward" (worsen) or
  "roll back" (improve/cure) month-over-month is called roll rate analysis.
  It is the foundation of Expected Credit Loss (ECL) modelling under Ind-AS 109.

--- OUTSTANDING_BALANCE ---

OUTSTANDING_BALANCE is the unpaid principal amount remaining on the loan account
as of the reporting date. It does not typically include accrued interest or
penalty charges (which are tracked separately). It represents the lender's
"at-risk" exposure on the account.

OUTSTANDING_BALANCE computation:
  Starting from SANCTION_AMOUNT, each EMI payment reduces the outstanding
  balance by the principal component of that EMI. Interest is charged
  separately on the reducing balance.

  Outstanding_Balance(month t) = Outstanding_Balance(month t-1)
                                 − Principal_Component(month t)

As VINTAGE_MONTHS increases, OUTSTANDING_BALANCE decreases (assuming regular
repayments). For defaulted loans, OUTSTANDING_BALANCE may include:
  - Unpaid principal
  - Accrued but unpaid interest
  - Penal charges
  (These are often disaggregated in collections systems)

OUTSTANDING_BALANCE in risk management:
  - Loss Given Default (LGD) = OUTSTANDING_BALANCE × (1 − Recovery Rate)
  - Recovery Rate for unsecured personal loans in India: typically 20%–50%
  - Recovery Rate for secured loans (home loan, LAP): 50%–80% after disposal
  - Provision Amount = PD × LGD × OUTSTANDING_BALANCE (ECL formula)
    where PD = Probability of Default

SECTION 8: DEFAULT AND WRITE-OFF FLAGS
================================================================================

--- LOAN_STATUS ---

LOAN_STATUS describes the current state of the loan account in its lifecycle.
Common LOAN_STATUS values:

  ACTIVE: Loan is currently being repaid. May be current (DPD=0) or overdue.

  CLOSED: Loan has been fully repaid. All principal and interest paid. Account
    is closed with a zero outstanding balance.

  NPA (Non-Performing Asset): Loan has crossed 90 DPD threshold. Under RBI
    classification, the loan is a sub-standard, doubtful, or loss asset.

  WRITTEN OFF: Lender has written off the loan from its books. 100% provision
    has been made. Recovery efforts may continue off-balance-sheet.

  RESTRUCTURED: Loan terms (EMI, tenure, rate) have been modified by mutual
    agreement between lender and borrower to help the borrower avoid default.
    Post-Covid-19, the RBI permitted one-time restructuring for eligible accounts.

  SETTLED: Borrower has paid a lump sum less than total outstanding (One-Time
    Settlement, OTS), and the lender has waived the remaining dues. Account
    is closed but CIBIL report shows "Settled" which is a negative tag.

  PRE-CLOSED: Borrower has repaid the full outstanding balance before the
    scheduled maturity date. Lenders may charge a prepayment penalty (typically
    2%–4% of outstanding principal) for fixed-rate loans.

  FORECLOSED: Used for secured loans where the lender has taken possession of
    the collateral (through SARFAESI or court order) and sold it to recover dues.

--- DEFAULT_FLAG ---

DEFAULT_FLAG is a binary variable (0 or 1, or False/True) indicating whether a
loan account has been classified as a default. This is the primary target
variable in credit risk machine learning models.

  DEFAULT_FLAG = 0: Borrower has not defaulted. Loan is performing.
  DEFAULT_FLAG = 1: Borrower has defaulted.

Definition of default varies by lender and regulatory framework:
  RBI Definition (90+ DPD): Most lenders in India define default as CURRENT_DPD
    exceeding 90 days (3 consecutive missed EMIs).
  Basel III Definition: More conservative; may include "unlikeliness to pay"
    criteria even before 90 DPD.
  Internal Definitions: Lenders may flag default at 60 DPD for early warning
    models, or use 30 DPD for certain high-risk segments.

DEFAULT_FLAG is derived from CURRENT_DPD, MAX_DPD, LOAN_STATUS, and
WRITE_OFF_FLAG. In a typical dataset:
  DEFAULT_FLAG = 1 if MAX_DPD >= 90 or LOAN_STATUS IN ('NPA','WRITTEN OFF','SETTLED')

Default Rate (DR):
  Portfolio Default Rate = Total accounts with DEFAULT_FLAG=1 / Total accounts × 100
  Typical default rates in Indian retail unsecured lending: 2%–8% annually
  depending on segment. Microfinance can range from 3%–15%.

Default rate by BUREAU_SCORE (approximate, Indian market):
  BUREAU_SCORE 750+: 1%–3% default rate
  BUREAU_SCORE 700–749: 3%–6%
  BUREAU_SCORE 650–699: 6%–12%
  BUREAU_SCORE 600–649: 12%–20%
  BUREAU_SCORE < 600 or NTC: 20%–40%

--- WRITE_OFF_FLAG ---

WRITE_OFF_FLAG is a binary variable (0 or 1) indicating whether a loan account
has been written off by the lender from its balance sheet.

  WRITE_OFF_FLAG = 0: Loan has not been written off.
  WRITE_OFF_FLAG = 1: Loan has been written off.

Write-off process:
  When a loan remains in NPA status (typically beyond 180 days overdue, or
  after a prescribed provisioning period), the lender makes a 100% provision
  against the outstanding balance and then writes off the loan. This removes
  the asset from the balance sheet but does not extinguish the legal obligation
  of the borrower — recovery efforts continue.

Write-off ≠ Waiver:
  A write-off is an accounting entry. The lender still pursues recovery through:
  - Recovery agents and collection agencies
  - Legal proceedings (civil courts, DRT)
  - SARFAESI Act (for secured loans): Allows banks and NBFCs to repossess and
    sell collateral without court intervention
  - Lok Adalat settlements (for smaller amounts)
  - Insolvency proceedings for large business loans

WRITE_OFF_FLAG relationship with DEFAULT_FLAG:
  All written-off loans are defaults (WRITE_OFF_FLAG=1 implies DEFAULT_FLAG=1).
  Not all defaults are written off (some defaults are recovered or restructured
  before write-off).

Regulatory provisioning leading to write-off:
  Sub-standard (90–365 DPD): 15% provision
  Doubtful-I (12-24 months NPA): 25% provision on secured, 100% on unsecured
  Doubtful-II (24-36 months NPA): 40% provision on secured, 100% on unsecured
  Doubtful-III (>36 months NPA): 100% provision on secured, 100% on unsecured
  Loss Asset: Full provision, candidates for write-off

SECTION 9: CREDIT RISK CONCEPTS AND RELATIONSHIPS
================================================================================

--- KEY RISK METRICS AND FORMULAS ---

FOIR (Fixed Obligation to Income Ratio):
  FOIR = (Sum of all monthly fixed obligations including new EMI) / Monthly Income
  Obligations include: all active loan EMIs, credit card minimum dues, other
  fixed commitments.
  Acceptable FOIR thresholds:
    Salaried: 40%–55%
    Self-Employed: 50%–65%
    High income (> ₹3L/month): Up to 70% (given higher absolute disposable income)

LTV (Loan-to-Value Ratio):
  LTV = LOAN_AMOUNT / Market Value of Collateral × 100
  RBI LTV caps for home loans:
    Loan ≤ ₹30 lakhs: Max 90% LTV
    Loan ₹30–75 lakhs: Max 80% LTV
    Loan > ₹75 lakhs: Max 75% LTV

EMI-to-Income Ratio:
  Single loan EMI / Monthly Income × 100
  Should typically not exceed 40% for a single loan.

Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR) — for business loans:
  DSCR = Net Operating Income / Total Debt Service
  Minimum acceptable DSCR: 1.25× (income covers debt 1.25 times)
  DSCR < 1.0 means the borrower cannot service debt from operations alone.

Expected Credit Loss (ECL) — Ind-AS 109:
  ECL = PD × LGD × EAD
  PD = Probability of Default (derived from BUREAU_SCORE, DPD history, vintage)
  LGD = Loss Given Default = (1 − Recovery Rate)
  EAD = Exposure at Default ≈ OUTSTANDING_BALANCE

--- RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN KEY VARIABLES ---

BUREAU_SCORE ↔ DEFAULT_FLAG:
  Strong negative correlation. Higher score → lower probability of default.
  BUREAU_SCORE is the single strongest predictor of default in underwriting.

MAX_DPD ↔ DEFAULT_FLAG:
  Direct relationship. MAX_DPD ≥ 90 typically defines DEFAULT_FLAG = 1.

VINTAGE_MONTHS ↔ CURRENT_DPD:
  Non-linear. Risk rises from month 0 to ~month 12, then gradually declines as
  loans are seasoned and high-risk borrowers have already defaulted.

MONTHLY_INCOME ↔ DEFAULT_FLAG:
  Moderate negative correlation. Higher income reduces default probability,
  but relationship weakens at high income levels (lifestyle inflation, etc.).

LOAN_AMOUNT ↔ DEFAULT_FLAG:
  Nuanced. Very small ticket loans (< ₹50K) can have high default in certain
  segments (e.g., microfinance). Very high ticket (> ₹10L) unsecured loans
  are carefully underwritten. Middle range shows moderate risk.

OCCUPATION ↔ DEFAULT_FLAG:
  Government salaried: Lowest default rates (~1%–2%)
  Large private salaried: Low default (~3%–5%)
  Self-employed professional: Moderate default (~4%–7%)
  Self-employed non-professional: Higher default (~7%–12%)

AGE ↔ DEFAULT_FLAG:
  Young borrowers (< 30) tend to have higher default rates due to income
  instability and shorter credit history. Middle-aged borrowers (35-50) show
  the lowest default rates. Very old borrowers (> 60) show moderate default
  risk due to health and income cessation.

SECTION 10: REGULATORY AND COMPLIANCE CONTEXT

--- RBI REGULATORY FRAMEWORK ---

The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) is the apex regulator for banks and NBFCs in
India. Key regulatory frameworks affecting loan portfolios:

IRACP (Income Recognition, Asset Classification and Provisioning) Norms:
  Govern how loans are classified as Standard, Sub-Standard, Doubtful, or Loss
  based on DPD. The 90-day NPA rule is the cornerstone.

Fair Practices Code (FPC):
  RBI mandates that lenders disclose all charges, interest rates, and terms
  transparently. Predatory lending and mis-selling are prohibited.

KYC (Know Your Customer) Norms:
  Lenders must verify borrower identity and address through approved documents
  (Aadhaar, PAN, Passport, Driving Licence, Voter ID) before extending credit.

CKYC (Central KYC):
  Central registry maintained by CERSAI. Once a borrower is CKYC-verified
  by one lender, the same KYC can be reused by other lenders.

Credit Bureau Reporting:
  All member lenders must report borrower credit information to licensed credit
  information companies (CICs) — CIBIL, Equifax, Experian, CRIF High Mark —
  monthly. Failure to report or inaccurate reporting is a regulatory violation.

SARFAESI Act 2002:
  Securitisation and Reconstruction of Financial Assets and Enforcement of
  Security Interest Act. Allows banks and NBFCs (with assets > ₹100 crore) to
  enforce collateral without court intervention for NPA accounts.

Debt Recovery Tribunal (DRT):
  Special quasi-judicial bodies for recovery of bank and NBFC debts above ₹20
  lakhs. Faster than civil courts but slower than SARFAESI.

--- CREDIT INFORMATION COMPANIES (BUREAUS) ---

CIBIL (TransUnion CIBIL):
  Founded 2000. India's oldest and most widely used credit bureau. CIBIL score
  (300–900) is the industry-standard. "Pull a CIBIL" is common parlance for
  any bureau inquiry.

Equifax India:
  Global bureau, operates in India since 2010. Used by many NBFCs and fintech
  lenders. Score range: 1–999.

Experian India:
  Global bureau, India operations since 2010. Widely used in retail lending
  and by credit card issuers.

CRIF High Mark:
  Specialised in MSME and microfinance bureau data. Important for lending to
  small businesses and self-help groups where other bureaus may have thin files.

Hard Inquiry vs. Soft Inquiry:
  Hard inquiry: Lender pulls a full credit report for underwriting a loan
    application. This is recorded and visible to other lenders. Multiple
    hard inquiries in a short period signal "credit hunger" and can reduce
    the bureau score.
  Soft inquiry: Borrower pulls their own report, or lender pulls for
    pre-screening. Not recorded as a derogatory event; does not affect score.

--- COLLECTION METHODS AND LEGAL FRAMEWORK ---

DPD and Resolution Strategies by Stage:

0 DPD (Current):
  Preventive: SMS reminders, email alerts, IVR calls 2-3 days before due date.

1–30 DPD (Bucket 1):
  Soft collections: Courtesy reminder calls, WhatsApp, email.
  Goal: Collect full EMI within the month ("cure within bucket").

31–60 DPD (Bucket 2):
  Hard collections: Dedicated calling agents, possible field visit for high
  ticket. Promise-to-Pay (PTP) tracking. Negotiated payment plans.
  Legal notices may be sent.

61–90 DPD (Bucket 3):
  Pre-NPA resolution: Intensive field visits, restructuring offers,
  One-Time Settlement (OTS) discussions. Lender wants to avoid NPA
  classification by collecting before 90 DPD.

91–180 DPD (NPA / Bucket 4+):
  NPA resolution: Hand off to specialised NPA unit or Recovery Agent (RA).
  Legal: Civil suit filing, DRT proceedings for large amounts.
  SARFAESI notice for secured loans.

>180 DPD (Write-Off Candidates):
  Write-off and continued recovery: Account removed from books.
  Assigned/sold to Asset Reconstruction Companies (ARC) at a discount.
  Recovery Agent pursues settlement.

One-Time Settlement (OTS):
  Lender agrees to accept a lump sum less than total outstanding dues to close
  the account. Example: Outstanding ₹2,00,000 → OTS accepted at ₹1,20,000.
  Borrower saves ₹80,000 but gets a "Settled" tag on CIBIL (negative marking).

Asset Reconstruction Company (ARC):
  NBFCs licensed by RBI under SARFAESI to acquire NPAs from banks/NBFCs at
  a discount and manage recovery. Example: Bank sells ₹1 crore NPA pool to
  ARC at ₹35 lakhs. ARC attempts recovery and keeps the upside.

--- USING LOAN DATASET FOR CREDIT RISK MODELLING ---

This dataset contains all the key features needed to build credit risk models.
Common modelling objectives:

1. Default Prediction (Binary Classification):
   Target: DEFAULT_FLAG (0 or 1)
   Features: BUREAU_SCORE, AGE, MONTHLY_INCOME, LOAN_AMOUNT, LOAN_TENURE,
     EMI_AMOUNT, INTEREST_RATE, OCCUPATION, STATE, GENDER, VINTAGE_MONTHS,
     CURRENT_DPD, MAX_DPD, OUTSTANDING_BALANCE, LOAN_PRODUCT
   Models: Logistic Regression, XGBoost, LightGBM, Random Forest, Neural Network

2. Write-Off Prediction (within the defaulted pool):
   Target: WRITE_OFF_FLAG (among accounts where DEFAULT_FLAG=1)
   This is a severity model — conditional on default, what is the probability
   of write-off (irrecoverable loss)?

3. Early Warning System (EWS):
   Predict which currently-current accounts (CURRENT_DPD=0) are at risk of
   becoming delinquent in the next 30/60/90 days.
   Uses: CURRENT_DPD trend, MAX_DPD, VINTAGE_MONTHS, BUREAU_SCORE,
     OUTSTANDING_BALANCE progression.

4. Collections Propensity Scoring:
   For delinquent accounts (DPD > 0), predict the probability that the
   borrower will make a payment in the next 30 days.
   Inputs: LAST_PAYMENT_DATE (days since), CURRENT_DPD, MAX_DPD,
     COLLECTION_BUCKET, payment history from monthly snapshots.

--- FEATURE ENGINEERING ---

From the raw dataset fields, useful derived features include:

EMI_to_Income = EMI_AMOUNT / MONTHLY_INCOME
  → Measures individual loan burden.

Sanction_Ratio = SANCTION_AMOUNT / LOAN_AMOUNT
  → 1.0 means fully approved; < 1.0 means counter-offered. Proxy for
    underwriting confidence.

LTV = LOAN_AMOUNT / SANCTION_AMOUNT [for asset-backed loans where sanction
  represents value of underlying asset]

Months_Since_Last_Payment = (Data Date − LAST_PAYMENT_DATE) in months
  → Higher = more delinquent or account is closed.

DPD_Flag_30 = 1 if CURRENT_DPD >= 30 else 0
DPD_Flag_60 = 1 if CURRENT_DPD >= 60 else 0
DPD_Flag_90 = 1 if CURRENT_DPD >= 90 else 0
  → One-hot-style DPD indicators for bucket membership.

Age_at_Maturity = AGE + LOAN_TENURE / 12
  → Should be < 65 for most products.

Remaining_EMIs = LOAN_TENURE − VINTAGE_MONTHS
  → Loans near maturity have different risk profile than newly disbursed loans.

--- DATA QUALITY CONSIDERATIONS ---

GENDER: Needs normalisation (as described above).
MONTHLY_INCOME: May have outliers (data entry errors producing ₹999,999,999).
  Use winsorization or log-transform.
BUREAU_SCORE: NTC borrowers may have NULL or -1. Impute carefully or create
  a separate indicator variable IS_NTC.
OUTSTANDING_BALANCE: May be 0 for closed loans. Filter to active/NPA loans
  for default modelling.
DISBURSAL_DATE / LAST_PAYMENT_DATE: Date parsing must handle DD-MM-YYYY,
  YYYY-MM-DD, and DD/MM/YYYY formats that may coexist in raw data.
STATE: Standardise to official state names; handle abbreviations (MH, KA, UP).
