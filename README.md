## Missing Value Handling

Instead of using traditional statistical imputation methods such as **mean** or **median**, missing values were estimated using **loan-specific business logic**. This preserves the relationship between financial variables and produces values that are more realistic for a lending dataset.

* **MONTHLY_INCOME:** Estimated from the customer's **EMI amount** using a fixed EMI-to-Income (FOIR) ratio based on the customer's occupation. For **defaulted** or **written-off** loans, income was set to **0**.
* **BUREAU_SCORE:** Estimated using only the same row's information, including **default/write-off status, payment delays (DPD), account vintage, collection bucket, loan status, interest rate, and EMI burden**, without relying on dataset-wide averages.
* **LAST_PAYMENT_DATE:** Estimated from the **disbursal date**, **loan tenure**, **account vintage**, **loan status**, and **delinquency indicators (DPD and collection bucket)** to approximate the most recent payment date.
* **OCCUPATION:** Missing values were replaced with **"Not Specified"**.
* **GENDER:** Standardized to **Male**, **Female**, or **Not Specified** for consistency.

This approach retains the financial context of each loan, avoids unrealistic average values, and generates imputations that better reflect real-world lending behaviour.


## Feature Engineering

The following features were engineered to improve the quality and consistency of summaries generated by the Small Language Model (SLM):

FOIR (EMI / Income): Measures repayment affordability and financial burden.
Credit Utilization: Indicates how much of the sanctioned amount is still outstanding.
Sanction Ratio: Shows the percentage of the requested loan that was approved.
Repayment Ratio: Estimates the proportion of the loan already repaid.
Disposable Income: Estimates the income remaining after EMI payments.
Tenure Progress & Months Left: Capture the loan lifecycle and repayment progress.
Delinquency Flags: Identify current and historical payment delays.
Payment Behaviour: Summarizes repayment history into business-friendly labels (e.g., Always Paid On Time, Recovered From Past Delays, Defaulted).
Risk Bands: Convert continuous values (Bureau Score, FOIR, Credit Utilization, DPD, Interest Rate) into interpretable categories for easier understanding.
Loan Health Score: Combines multiple risk signals (default, write-off, delinquency, bureau score, FOIR, utilization) into a single overall health metric.
Account Health & Risk Tier: Provide an overall assessment of customer credit risk.
Primary Risk: Identifies the dominant reason contributing to the customer's risk profile.

## Fine-Tuning Method

### Why LoRA?

Standard full fine-tuning updates every weight in the model, which is expensive and causes **catastrophic forgetting** — the model loses its general reasoning ability while learning the new task. LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) sidesteps this by freezing the base model and injecting small trainable adapter layers. This means:

- Training uses a fraction of the GPU memory and compute
- The base model's language understanding stays intact
- Adapters can be swapped or merged without touching the original weights


### Why CPT before SFT?

Plain LoRA SFT (training on Q&A pairs) only teaches the model **structure** — how to format an answer, what tone to use, how to follow instructions. It does not teach it facts it doesn't already know.

For a domain like loan underwriting, the model has little pre-existing knowledge of Indian credit bureau scoring, DPD buckets, FOIR thresholds, or NPA classification rules. If you fine-tune directly on Q&A pairs, the model learns to sound like an expert without actually becoming one.

**CPT (Continued Pre-Training) with LoRA** fixes this. Instead of Q&A pairs, CPT trains the model on raw domain text — definitions, rules, relationships, worked examples — using a causal language modelling objective (predict the next token). This injects domain knowledge directly into the LoRA layers before any instruction tuning begins.

The two-phase flow:

Phase 1 — CPT:   Raw domain text  →  LoRA learns domain knowledge
Phase 2 — SFT:   Q&A pairs        →  LoRA learns to answer questions using that knowledge


Both phases train the **same LoRA adapter**, so knowledge from CPT is retained and built upon during SFT.


### Data Strategy

The quality and variety of training data matters more than the number of epochs. Repeating the same examples over many epochs leads to memorisation of phrasing rather than generalisation.

Better alternatives:

- **Paraphrasing:** Each training example can be rewritten 2–4× with different phrasing, sentence structure, or framing. The model sees the same underlying knowledge expressed differently, which forces it to learn the concept rather than the exact wording.
- **Data replication with variation:** For Q&A pairs, the question can be prefixed or rephrased ("According to the report...", "Based on the data...", "What does the analysis say about...") to create natural variation without fabricating new answers.

This approach is more effective than simply increasing epochs, and reduces the risk of overfitting to surface patterns.


## Evaluation Strategy

### 1. Summarization

| Metric | Why it is Used | Limitation |
|--------|----------------|------------|
| **ROUGE** | Measures lexical overlap between the generated summary and the reference summary using **Precision**, **Recall**, and **F1-score**. It effectively evaluates content coverage. | Does not capture semantic similarity, synonyms, or factual correctness. |
| **BERTScore** | Measures semantic similarity using contextual embeddings, making it more robust to paraphrasing and synonyms than ROUGE. | Does not reliably detect factual inconsistencies or hallucinations. |
| **LLM-as-a-Judge** | Evaluates summaries based on semantic quality, coherence, and factual accuracy, addressing limitations of ROUGE and BERTScore. | Can be expensive to run and may introduce evaluation bias depending on the judge model. |

### 2. Recommendation & Risk Prediction

| Metric | Purpose |
|--------|---------|
| **Recall** | Measures the model's ability to identify all relevant items or positive cases, ensuring important recommendations or high-risk instances are not missed. |
| **Precision** | Measures the accuracy of positive predictions, ensuring that recommended items or predicted high-risk cases are truly relevant. |
| **F1-score** | Provides a balanced evaluation by combining Precision and Recall into a single metric, making it useful when both are equally important. |
